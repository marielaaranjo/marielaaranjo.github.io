What is a Routing Number?

In the world of banking, a routing number is a nine-digit numerical code that is used to identify a specific financial institution. It serves as an address for directing funds during transactions, such as electronic transfers, wire transfers, and direct deposits. Think of it as a postal code for your bank, indicating where money should be sent or received. Each bank or credit union has its unique routing number, allowing for seamless and secure transactions.

Structure of a Routing Number

A routing number consists of three parts: the first four digits represent the Federal Reserve routing symbol, followed by four digits representing the American Bankers Association (ABA) institution identifier, and the final digit, known as the check digit, is used for verification purposes. This structure ensures accuracy and security in financial transactions.
